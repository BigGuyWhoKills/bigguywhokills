Now, the guns that I own.

This is my Winchester 1300 Defender. It has an 18" barrel (the shortest legal barrel length), holds 8 rounds (7+1 of 2-3/4"), and can chamber both 2-3/4" and 3" 12 gauge shells including slugs and steel shot. I take good care of it by storing it properly and cleaning it after each use. I have a 9 round holding sling mounted to it, and I'm currently looking for a flashlight mount for a high output flashlight (but I'm about to give up and order a new slide with a built in light). I have considered buying a pistol grip for it, but the stock is just too useful for clay pigeons (can hardly miss at 20 yards). I think that I will instead get a pistol grip stock, so I get the control of a pistol grip, without losing a full length stock. I have two 25 round belt shell holders that I use when clay pigeon hunting, and all in all it's a fun setup.


My Ruger 10/22

I bought the above gun several years ago.  It is a Ruger K10/22T.  Above is a picture of what it looks like (except that I have a Bushnell Sportview 3-9x40 silver matte finish scope mounted on it and a basic sling), and I plan on making some major modifications.  The only modification that I've done so far is a magazine release extension.  The other modifications that I am planning on are a stainless steel target barrel, a thumbhole laminate stock, a 2# trigger, and the slide release modification.  My friend Kevin has the same basic version of this rifle (above), and it is a true winner with solid performance.

I also own a Heckler and Koch USP .45 (purchased 12 June 1998).  This is an exceptional weapon that is as reliable as a autoloading handgun can get.  It was a bit pricey, but so are most other guns of this caliber and quality.
When I was in the market for a pistol, I know that I wanted a large caliber handgun.  After some research, I added some more requirements: single/double action, frame mounted safety, .40/.45/10mm (9mm was never an option), and an external hammer.  The external hammer was the one thing that set my mind on a HK or a Sig.  That was the only thing that put Glock out of the running. A few months earlier, HK had won the SOCOM competition for the new special forces primary handgun.  The HK MK 23 beat out Sig, Glock, and Browning.
Now I needed to decide on a caliber. The 10mm was new on the scene, and the FBI had many pistols and submachine guns fielded in this new round.  But studies and field results were showing that this round had too much penetrating power, and would often carry on through the intended target, and cause unintended damage.
Since my primary use of this weapon was for self defence, this was an unnaceptable risk.  The .40 caliber round was fairly new, and was generating favorable reviews.  But at the time it had not proved itself as well as the .45 caliber.  That, and the fact that the .45 was the current king in stopping power, set my decision on the HK USP .45 (fullsize).  Every stopping power comparison that I have seen since has put the .45 on top.  I don't care about lab test either.  The only test that I am willing to consider are real world shootings.  These results are taken from police and coroner reports of personal defense, military, and other similar shootings.
In the last report that I saw, CCI .45 Gold Dot's were the best round in terms of real world stopping power.  That is the load that I currently use.

This is my Bushmaster AR15
