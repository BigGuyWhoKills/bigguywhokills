Programming

Commodore 64: I started programming in the 80's, on the family Commodore 64.  Originally, we had no non-volatile storage. So after programming for dozens of minutes and running my program, when I turned the computer off that program was gone.  Eventually we got a tape drive.  Not a reel to reel, but a 4-track cassette tape deck.  It used blank audio tapes to save data to.  You kept track of where data was by its counter position.  Remember the little speedometer counters on your boom box?  We had to write that number down on the cassette liner, and make sure the counter was reset when the tape was fully rewound.  I do not remember if it was double-sided or not.

Apple II: When I started 5th grade, our small school in Washington bought 5 Apple II computers.  Coincidentally, these were kept in our classroom.  This allowed me to have a lot of access to these computers, and to program with them.  By the end of the year, I was at about the limit of what I thought could be done with text, and was ready to start graphical work.

Macintosh: When my brother went to college, he bought a 128k Macintosh.  I did not program on that, but it introduced me to my first graphical OS.

Linux: In my senior year of high school, I was able to take a programming class that used Unix workstations connected to a server.  This was my first work in C.  I did well, but did not retain much due to not programming for several years.

Wintel: Also during my senior year, I was able to play games on a friend's computer.  While I did not write any programs, it taught me a little bit of MS DOS and Windows 3.x.

I received my first PC as a gift from my friend Jon's father.  It was a 286 with half of it's memory soldered to the motherboard, and half in 14 pin DIP sockets.  I relentlessly upgraded that beast, a few parts at a time, as finances allowed.

My first real programming class was a Programming for Electrical Engineers class at Utah Valley State College (now Utah Valley University).  That class covered C.  My final project for that class was a simple sprite game that utilized Borland Graphics Interface to draw your player character and a monster that chased you.  There was no way to win.  A later semester covered Intel Assembly Language.


I have studied C, C++, C#, Assembly, JavaScript, and am currently studying Java (fall 2015).  I have taught myself a bit of Basic as a teenager, but I do not consider myself proficient in it.
